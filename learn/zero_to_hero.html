Part A · How the brain works

First, what is a neural network?

Forget the sci-fi. It's a voting machine. You feed in facts about a stock today; it adds up weighted votes; it announces a verdict. That's the whole idea — everything else is detail.

facts  →  weighted votes  →  verdict

RSI, momentum, volume…  →  each fact pushes for/against each answer  →  BUY / SELL / HOLD

Level 1

The simple network (no hidden layer)

Two rows of nodes (a node just holds a number). Inputs on the left (the facts), outputs on the right (the verdicts). Every input connects to every output by a wire, and each wire has a weight — how strongly that fact votes for that verdict.

Yellow = the wires feeding the BUY node. It adds up fact × weight for every input, plus a bias.

The maths is just adding up

Each output = (fact₁×w₁) + (fact₂×w₂) + … + bias. Big positive weight = "this fact strongly votes for me". Negative = "votes against".

Softmax → a decision

A final step turns the raw scores into percentages that add to 100% — e.g. BUY 80%, HOLD 15%, SELL 5% — and the machine picks the biggest. Done.

⚠️ The limit: a simple net can only learn flat rules like "more RSI → more BUY". It can't learn "low RSI is good only if the trend is also up". For that, you need…
Level 2

Adding a brain: the hidden layer

Slip a middle row of nodes in between — the hidden layer. Each middle node is a scout: it watches all the facts and "lights up" only for a specific pattern. Training teaches each scout what to specialise in.

🔦 Scout A learns…

"RSI is low AND trend is up" → an oversold-dip-in-an-uptrend detector. (That's literally the pullback we tested!)

🔦 Scout B learns…

"Volume was heavy AND is now fading" → a selling-exhaustion detector.

The verdicts then vote on the scouts' reports instead of raw facts. The "lighting up" step (a function called ReLU or tanh) is the secret — without it, stacking layers collapses back to a simple net. Stack more hidden layers and you get deep learning: patterns-of-patterns.

Level 3

How it learns: downhill & blame

Training = nudging all the wire-numbers until the verdicts match what actually made money. Two ideas:

1

Measure how wrong it is

One number — the error. Truth was "BUY" but it said BUY 30%? Big error. Said BUY 90%? Small error.

2

Walk downhill (gradient descent)

Picture every wire-setting as a spot on a landscape where height = how wrong you are. Feel which way is downhill and take a small step. Repeat thousands of times → you slide into a valley of low error.

3

Pass the blame back (backpropagation)

The output was wrong — so ask each scout "how much was your fault?", then each wire feeding it, all the way back to the inputs. Each wire then knows which way to nudge. That backward blame-trace is backprop.

📁 In our code this engine is microgix.py — every number remembers how it was built, so .backward() can trace the blame. You built a tiny copy of it yourself in learn/tiny_nn.py.
Level 4 · Proof

That gap is the entire reason hidden layers exist — when the pattern is real and nonlinear. Hold that thought.

Level 5

Our actual trading network

It's exactly the picture above, just bigger: 32 input nodes → 5 verdicts (BUY / SELL / HOLD / AVOID / WATCHLIST), about 165 weights in total. In code: LogisticGate in gating_model.py, trained by train_gate.

The 32 facts it sees

RSI, MACD, Bollinger %B, momentum (20d/63d), volume ratio, candlestick shape, distance-from-low/high, the rubric's own score… each normalised to a small number.

The 5 verdicts

It outputs a probability for each, picks the biggest. A promotion ladder decides whether it's allowed to act on that verdict or just watch in the shadows.

Part B · The hunt for an edge

So… can it actually make money?

We fed it real daily prices from Yahoo (open/high/low/close/volume, split-adjusted) and tested honestly — out-of-sample, with random-entry controls, and non-overlapping trades. First, the experiments that came up empty:

❌
Pure technicals, broadly. The NN's picks averaged +0.33% vs just buying anything +1.23% (20-day). Worse than buy-and-hold.
⚠️
Earnings-beat drift. Looked promising (+1.5%), but ~half was the untradeable 2020 COVID rebound; stripped out, it's a thin ~+0.5%.
🪤
The pullback "mirage". A naive test showed a big edge — then a proper random-entry control shrank it to +0.04–0.5%. Most of the "edge" was just market drift.
🧪 The lesson that saved us from fooling ourselves: always compare to a random-entry control and use independent (non-overlapping) trades. Without them, you manufacture fake edges.
The real find

This is textbook mean-reversion: where there's more movement, there's more dip to revert. A real, consistent, theory-backed signal — strongest exactly where you'd expect.

But honesty first: it beats random, not buy-and-hold. It trades rarely and sits in cash, so it can't out-return simply holding a stock that 5×'d. Its value is timing & risk, not more money than holding.
The half that matters most

Risk management = survival

The entry is only half a strategy. Real traders live on the exit. Adding an ATR stop-loss (cut losers fast) transformed the risk, sized at 1% of capital per trade ("R" = risk-units):

Worst single tradeMax drawdown
−4.4R  💥 account-killer  →  −1.1R ✅ capped buy & hold −69%  →  managed ~−12%

The stop didn't add raw profit — it removed the disasters. No single trade can blow you up, and the equity curve becomes something you can sleep through.

🪦 The one thing we can't fully test on free data: stops protect against stocks that dip and go to zero — and "survivor data" (Yahoo) has deleted those companies from history. So risk-management's true value is understated here.
Part C · The honest verdict

What it all adds up to

✅
The pullback entry is a real edge — beats random on ~3 of 4 S&P 500 names, biggest in volatile stocks (+2 to +7pp/trade).
✅
Risk management works — ATR stops cap the tail and cut drawdowns to a fraction of buy-and-hold.
❌
It doesn't out-return buy-and-hold — it's a timing / risk-adjusted edge, not a money-printer.
🧠
Simpler beats deeper. The hidden layer never beat the plain linear model — even after we gave the scouts the time dimension (it degenerated to firing zero trades). The edge here is genuinely simple.

The big takeaway — and it surprised us

We kept hoping for a clever deep network. The data said the opposite: a linear model, on a handful of well-chosen features, on volatile names, with disciplined stops. The levers were never network depth — they were which stocks and risk control. The fancy brain was the least important part.

Why depth didn't help: remember XOR — a hidden layer only wins when there's a real nonlinear pattern to find. Here there wasn't one; the edge is a simple "deeply oversold & turning up → modest bounce". The line already captured it.
